# Chapter 7 Opening Vignette — The Simulation That Learns

*Draft. Book voice, not paper voice. Guy Ritchie register.*
*Cast: Turkish (sharp, Statham), Tommy (earnest factory manager), Propček (the Czech outsider with the briefcase).*

---

Propček arrives on a Tuesday.

Nobody asked for him. Nobody knows who sent him. He shows up at the
warehouse with a briefcase, a Czech accent, and a habit of asking
questions nobody asked him to ask. He says he is here to test the
forklift.

Turkish looks at Tommy. Tommy looks at Turkish. Tommy says: "We've
tested the forklift."

"A hundred and fourteen times," Turkish confirms, without taking his
eyes off Propček. "Picked up the pallet, moved the pallet, put the
pallet down. A hundred and fourteen times. No complaints."

Tommy nods. A hundred and fourteen times is a lot of times.

"That's a lot of times," Tommy says.

Propček opens the briefcase. Inside are not documents. Inside are
sequences. Hundreds of them. Random operations — pick up, put down,
pick up, put down, but in orders that no reasonable forklift driver
would ever attempt. Some sequences try to put down a pallet that was
never picked up. Some try to pick up two pallets at once. Some idle
the forklift for thirty steps and then attempt a release at full speed.

Tommy frowns. "Nobody drives a forklift like that."

"That's the point, Tommy," Turkish says quietly.

The factory doesn't fail on Tuesday afternoon when Dave is driving. The
factory fails on the Saturday night shift when the temp tries something
Dave would never try, and the forklift does something nobody knew it
could do.

Propček hands Tommy a sequence. Four operations.

    Initialize forklift. Capacity: 3 pallets.
    Pick up pallet #1.
    Put down pallet #1.
    Put down pallet #3.

Tommy reads it twice. "Pallet #3 was never picked up."

"Run it," says Turkish.

Tommy runs the sequence. The forklift accepts the spurious put-down
without complaint. The odometer, which counts pick-ups and put-downs
separately, now reads: picked up 1, put down 2. More pallets put down
than picked up. An impossibility that will propagate silently into every
shift report, every inventory count, every quarterly audit until someone
notices that the numbers don't add up and nobody can explain why.

Tommy stares at the odometer.

"How did he find that?"

Turkish is already lighting a cigarette. "He didn't find it, Tommy. He
generated seven hundred sequences. The forklift found it. He just
watched."

"But we tested it a hundred and fourteen times—"

"We tested it a hundred and fourteen times the way *Dave* drives."
Turkish exhales. "Propček tested it seven hundred times the way nobody
drives. The law doesn't care how Dave drives. The law cares about the
odometer."

Tommy looks at the odometer again. Picked up 1. Put down 2.

In some parts of the world, people roll the odometer back to make the
machine look younger than it is. The numbers look right. The history
is a lie. Here the odometer has been rolled forward — past a number
that never happened — and the effect is the same. The audit trail
says two pallets were put down. One pallet exists. The discrepancy
will travel through every report that trusts the odometer until
someone arrives with a briefcase and no small talk and asks to see
the original four lines.

"So what do we do?"

Turkish takes the paper from Propček. Reads the four lines. Crosses out
the first and last. Holds up the middle two.

"We fix the forklift so it can't put down what it never picked up. And
then we let Propček run his seven hundred sequences again until the
odometer never lies."

Propček closes the briefcase. He will be back on Wednesday.

---

This, translated out of a Bermondsey warehouse and into Elixir, is
property-based testing.

The forklift is `Sim.Resource` — the capacity-limited server at the
heart of every queuing model in sim\_ex. The odometer is the statistics
struct: arrivals, departures, grants, releases, busy time. The
sequences are `proper_statem` command lists, generated by PropEr's
state machine model and shrunk to the minimal reproduction when a
postcondition fails.

The four-operation sequence is real. It was found during development
of sim\_ex by the `Sim.Statham.Resource` model — three hundred random
command sequences, executed in one hundred and thirty-five milliseconds.
The postcondition `grants >= releases` caught the failure. The existing
test suite — over a hundred point tests and several hundred property
trials — had not.

The Czech gentleman's name is PropCheck — Klaus Alfert's Elixir wrapper
around Kostis Sagonas's PropEr library. Turkish is the senior engineer
who already suspected the edge cases were there but didn't have the
tool to find them systematically. Tommy is the factory manager who
thought a hundred and fourteen tests meant the forklift was safe.
And `proper_statem`, which we call `proper_statham` in the codebase —
because the engine should be indestructible and the package should
always be delivered.

On the shop floor, this could never happen. You cannot put down a pallet
you never picked up. The impossibility is enforced by gravity, by mass,
by the fact that matter exists. The physical world has conservation laws,
and it enforces them for free — no auditor required.

A simulation has no gravity. Entities are numbers. Events are function
calls. The odometer will happily read negative if nobody told it not to.
Joe Armstrong — who built the machine this simulation runs on — was a
physicist before he was a programmer. He understood that isolated systems
conserve, that failures are structural, that recovery must be designed
in. His runtime has those instincts. The simulation that runs on his
runtime does not.

Propček's briefcase is the substitute. Seven hundred sequences, checked
against a postcondition that says `picked_up >= put_down`. That
postcondition is the conservation law the simulation doesn't have. It is
the physics the software lacks. It is what gravity does for the shop
floor, done instead by a Czech gentleman with a briefcase and no small
talk.

You have already seen informed priors. In Chapter 2, we told eXMC that
service times are positive and that the cycle frequency is probably
around twenty quarters. Those were priors about *inputs* — what values
are plausible before we see data. Propček's postconditions are priors
about *behavior* — what the system can and cannot do regardless of
inputs. Same idea, different layer. The prior says "I know something
about the world." The postcondition says "I know something about what
the world cannot do." Both constrain the machine to the region where
physics lives. An uninformed prior lets the posterior wander into
negative service times. A missing postcondition lets the odometer wander
into negative pallets. Both are the same mistake: forgetting to tell the
machine what you already know.

The rest of this chapter is about what happens when Propček arrives at
your factory. What he tests. What he finds. What you do about it. And
why the four-line diagnosis is worth more than the hundred-line test
suite — because the four lines tell you exactly which pallet was never
picked up.
